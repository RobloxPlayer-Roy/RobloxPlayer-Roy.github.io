February 15, 1932

Today was my first day on the front lines. The reality of war hit me harder than I ever imagined. We woke before dawn, the cold biting through our uniforms. After a quick meal, we marched to our assigned position. The landscape was bleak, a desolate wasteland scarred by trenches and craters. I could feel the tension among the men as we neared the front. The distant gunfire from last night was now an almost constant roar. We were briefed by Sergeant Miller, a grizzled veteran who had seen more battles than he cared to recount. His voice was steady, but the gravity of his words was unmistakable. We were to hold our position at all costs. My hands were trembling as I gripped my rifle. Jack and Sam were nearby, their faces pale but resolute. There wasn’t much time for talk. The Afrar forces were already advancing. I remember the first shots fired, the chaos that ensued. The noise was deafening, a cacophony of gunfire, explosions, and the cries of the wounded. I shot my rifle for the first time today, aiming at shadows and figures in the distance. It's a sickening feeling, knowing that each pull of the trigger could end a life. But there’s no room for hesitation in battle. It's kill or be killed. I tried to focus on my training, but nothing could have prepared me for this. We managed to hold our ground, but it came at a cost. I saw men fall beside me, friends I had only just met. The medics were overwhelmed, doing their best to save lives in the midst of the carnage. I helped carry the wounded back to safety, their screams haunting my every step. As night fell, the fighting subsided. The silence that followed was almost more unnerving than the battle itself. The battlefield was strewn with the aftermath of the day's conflict. We spent hours fortifying our position, knowing that the Afrar could attack again at any moment. I'm exhausted, both physically and emotionally. My hands are blistered and my body aches, but the worst pain is the one in my heart. I think of home often, of the farm and the simple life I once knew. It feels like a distant memory now. As I write this, the sky is dark, and the stars seem indifferent to our suffering. I pray for strength, for the courage to face another day. I don't know what tomorrow will bring, but I have to believe that we are fighting for a better future.

Private Thomas Twain, signing off for the night.
